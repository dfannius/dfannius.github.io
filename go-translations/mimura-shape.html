Preface

"Learn good moves and bad moves through visual shapes. That alone is enough to win at Go."

This is that kind of book. Even if you don’t understand the logic behind it, please trust it and give it a try.

The first edition was published in 2006, already [?] years ago. After that, a revised and expanded "Complete Edition" came out in 2012, and now I have been given the opportunity to make revisions a second time. Given that it has quietly managed to survive, it seems to be a surprisingly effective book.

In 2016, I also taught a television lecture series on the topic of "stone shapes," and I feel my instruction on "stone shapes" has matured a little. I believe this is reflected in the practice problems added this time, so please enjoy them.

From the latter half of 2016, a great revolution in Go brought about by artificial intelligence took place. In particular, the standards for good and bad moves in the opening have changed more dramatically than at any point in Go history.

In the Go that AI plays, there are many moves that ignore stone shape - playing many stones on the second line without hesitation, or allowing the opponent to make hane over the head of two stones. I was truly astonished.

The style of play that AI and professional players pursue - minimizing defense and maximizing efficiency - is supported by enormous reading ability. Do not be misled by this: let us learn stone shapes in the traditional way. That means mastering correct play and proper defense.

For players up to about 5-dan, if you make an effort to play correct moves and moves with good shape, you will find it easier to win, and will suffer less from being unable to read ahead.

I hope that through this book, your Go will become more and more enjoyable.

November 2018 Mimura Tomoyasu, 9-dan

Why You Should Learn Shape

The value of correct shape always becomes apparent after a few to a dozen or so moves. Conversely, bad shape will without fail become a burden once the position has advanced a little.

We professional players do not always decide on our moves by reading out all possible future variations in every situation. Rather, in most cases we judge "shape" by feel, thinking that a move with bad shape must be bad, and that playing correct shape cannot turn out wrong.

Trying to resolve everything through reading and calculation is exhausting — and that is difficult even for us. All the more so, if amateur players try to reason through everything logically, they will find themselves thoroughly worn out just from playing a single game.

Developing a feel for "shape" leads to eliminating wasteful reading. Since you won’t be expending energy on pointless calculation, your concentration at critical moments will also improve.

And it is not only your results that will benefit. Since you will be able to play faster and more easily, you will spend less time in endless deliberation, and your opponents will surely appreciate it.

Maintaining correct shape and avoiding bad shape is an important fundamental. Since the ideal is to grasp "good shape" and "bad shape" by feel rather than by logic, this book will introduce many examples of both. As for the reasons why shapes are good or bad, I will explain them at a relaxed pace while looking at a variety of shapes in practice.

How to Use This Book

In order to help you learn "shape" by eye, this book marks diagrams showing "good shape" with a :) and diagrams showing "bad shape" with a :(. Diagrams marked with three :(:(:( in a row show particularly terrible shape. Those marked with two :(:( are also shapes you should avoid whenever possible.

Most of the theme diagrams are presented as problems. Please think about the next move as if you were facing that position in one of your own games.

Deep reading is not required. First, check whether what catches your eye is "good shape" or "bad shape."

The Split Shape
Diagram 1

If Black ignores White’s attachment at 1 and plays elsewhere at 2, White will push through at 3. This is the "split shape" - the bad shape one must above all avoid.

There are several bad shapes, but most of them are bad in the sense of being inefficient. Since Go is a game that competes on the efficiency of stones, wasteful stones are said to be "bad shape." But even worse than that is the split shape.

The more you play moves that lead to a split shape, the more you damage your previously played stones. Even if a bad shape is somewhat inefficient, it is still slightly better than having no stone there at all. But the split shape actually turns your previously played moves into a liability.

The bad shape I want you to leave behind first and foremost is the split shape.

Diagram 2

"I understand well that Diagram 1 is bad for Black. But unless White is playing a ko threat, of course Black will respond to White 1 in Diagram 2 with Black 2. Something like Diagram 1 won’t happen."

Many people think this way, and indeed that is correct. However, in preparing to publish this book, I reviewed a considerable number of game records of amateur players on internet game sites and elsewhere, and found that split shapes were being played far more frequently than I had imagined. I truly felt that simply developing the habit of avoiding the split shape would certainly make a player 1 or 2 ranks stronger.

So why do people end up playing the split shape? Let us think about this together while looking at real examples.
