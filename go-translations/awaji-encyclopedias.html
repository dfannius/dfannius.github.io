Foreword

"Invasions" and "endgame." These are two distinct fields, but they have an affinity in the sense that concentrated study of the shapes that arise frequently in actual games lets you improve your strength in a short time. This book therefore classifies these two fields shape by shape and organizes them in encyclopedia form, so that you can refer to them whenever you like.

Part 1 takes up the invasion shapes that appear frequently in actual games. The fighting around an invasion is a key point of the middlegame. One comment I often hear from readers is how frustrating it is to have a spot they thought was settled territory easily ravaged. So, taking mainly the perspective of the side being invaded, I have used a number—a "danger level"—to show clearly just how safe each shape is. I also discuss recommended variations and ways to defend, so this will surely prove useful in your own games.

Part 2 covers what is known as the large endgame—endgame plays worth ten points or more.

3

Foreword (continued)

Because the endgame is an important field that directly determines who wins and who loses, I myself still study it to this day. Professionals calculate down to precise point counts, but most amateurs have no need to go that far. First, let us learn to tell apart the thirty-point endgame and the fifteen-point endgame introduced in this book. Even that alone should reliably change your winning percentage. At the back of the book I have compiled the sizes of endgame plays into a table, so I recommend referring to it repeatedly until you can grasp the relative size of endgame plays intuitively.

I would be glad if this book raises your middlegame and endgame strength and becomes an occasion for you to grow even fonder of Go.

June 2015 Awaji Shuzo, 9-dan

Introduction: How to Think About the Large Endgame

To begin, allow me to offer four points for getting stronger at the endgame.

  1. Merely following your opponent’s moves is no good
  2. Know the size of endgame plays
  3. Endgame plays involving the strength or weakness of stones are big
  4. The merits and demerits of sente
“Know the Size of Endgame Plays”

This one, first of all, almost goes without saying. If you don’t know the size of endgame plays, you’ll end up rattling off one small move after another.

That said—as I mentioned in the foreword—you amateurs have absolutely no need to think down to "such-and-such a fraction of a point." It is enough to be able to tell a thirty-point endgame play from a fifteen-point one.

If you play sloppily, without knowing the size of endgame plays, you’ll easily lose twenty or thirty points. Just being able to hold that loss to five or six points should sharply boost your winning percentage.

In this book, rather than have you memorize precise point counts, I have expressed the size of endgame plays by a number of stars (★) instead of a point count, so that you learn instead which endgame plays are big. One star is worth about five points, but you need not think about precise point counts. Strictly speaking, even two plays both rated three stars will differ by a slight margin, but a difference of that

order is nothing to worry about.

When a six-star endgame play is available, make a point of not playing a three-star one. Even that alone, I think, will change your endgame.

Furthermore, I have tried wherever possible to introduce the follow-up nerai after an endgame play and the tesuji of endgame technique, so once you master this book, your all-around endgame strength should improve as well.

Also, for sente endgame plays and reverse-sente plays, I have not simply set the value at double the point count. It depends on the circumstances of the sente or reverse-sente play, but I have evaluated them at roughly one and a half to two times the value.

“Merely Following Your Opponent’s Moves Is No Good”

This is something I emphasize quite strongly throughout the book.

In the endgame, the more moves you play, the smaller they often become. For example, in Diagram 1, as long as you keep answering White 1 and 3, the hane-and-connect, with Black 4 without a second thought, your endgame will never get stronger.

“The Merits and Demerits of Sente”

Sente is certainly appealing. Even when counting the point value of an endgame play, for a rough estimate we double the point count.

But please be aware that sente also has its downside.

In the endgame—though this is not limited to the endgame—it is not the case that you may play anything at all so long as it is sente.

For example, in Diagram 8, White 1 and 3, the hane-and-connect, are sente, but the exchange for Black 2 and 4 has solidified Black on the lower side. This is an extreme example, but White 1 and 3, sente though they may be, are a serious minus.
