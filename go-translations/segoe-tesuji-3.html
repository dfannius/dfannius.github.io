Other Names for Go

Go has many other names: zuòyǐn (sitting in seclusion), shǒután (conversing with the hands), fāngyuán (square and round), làn kē (the rotted axe handle), niǎolù zhī zhēng (the contention of crows and egrets), and others.

The Shishuo Xinyu says: "Wang Zhonglang called Go zuòyǐn; Zhi Daolin called it shǒután." Both zuòyǐn and shǒután mean playing Go.

During the Meiji era, Murase Shuho founded the Hoensha (方圓社); the book Zuoyin Congtan records this fact in Japanese Go history.

The "fāng" (square) in fāngyuán represents the four corners of the board, while the stones are round in shape — hence the name "square and round."

Shǒután is a particularly evocative term. Its meaning is that through Go one may find a lifelong kindred spirit.

Philosophy of Go
  1. One must have intuition; first seek experience.
  2. In all one’s actions, first seek to know your opponent.
  3. Attack urgently and then pull back; danger is self-invited.
  4. Know when to stop — remember this well.
  5. When the best has been exhausted, discard the rest.
  6. Use attack to build territory; take all the advantages.
  7. To lose by one point is no different from losing by resignation — aim for decisive play.
  8. See the game with your eyes; understand the game with your mind.
  9. The way of Go prizes rectitude; be neither greedy nor timid.
  10. The art has no limit; when it comes to virtue, do not yield — this is most precious.
  11. Win and face criticism, lose and face slander — such things are heard from time to time.
  12. Having entered through the gate, seek refinement and depth, with no wasted effort and no regrets.
  13. The ultimate subtleties cannot be passed down or taught — one must simply awaken to the Way.
  14. Go has action and stillness; the subtlety lies in the heart. My path ahead is vast.
