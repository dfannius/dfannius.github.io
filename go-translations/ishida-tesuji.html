Preface

There is no need to explain to those of you who have picked up this book that tesuji are important in go. They appear in every phase of the game from opening to endgame, and it is not uncommon for knowledge of tesuji to decide the outcome of a game. Fortunately, however, the more tesuji you learn, the more directly it translates to improvement, making this an accessible field for amateur players.

This book contains 668 diagrams of tesuji patterns and tactical patterns that frequently arise in actual games. You may memorize them one by one from start to finish, cover the solution diagrams and work through them as problems, or use the book to review games you have played — any approach works.

One word of caution: some diagrams have long sequences, and some include difficult variations. When you encounter such diagrams, I encourage you to read the explanations while setting up the position on a board. It is essential to review again and again until the knowledge becomes second nature.

The variation diagrams and reference diagrams also contain important tesuji, so I am confident that every diagram in the book has something to teach. I am certain that if you make good use of this book, your strength at go will improve.

April 2018

Honinbo Shuho, 24th

Editor’s Preface

Compiling a go encyclopedia is an extremely difficult undertaking. If one arranges entries in order of coordinate notation, for example, the relationships between diagrams are lost. Classification by shape seems desirable, but one quickly runs into situations where classification becomes impossible.

The complications are endless, from the essentials down to the finest details, and no one would believe that a perfectly flawless encyclopedia could be compiled despite all of them.

Setting aside the ideal, in editing this book I had no choice but to proceed through repeated trial and error. It goes without saying that one cannot create an index without first arranging the diagrams — yet one cannot arrange the diagrams without first creating an index. Reaching a resolution required many twists and turns.

Even so, there were many diagrams that could not be classified by shape alone, and I found myself having to classify cutting shapes by a separate criterion.

Adopting a dual standard is no more than an unavoidable measure, but it makes reasonable lookup possible and seems to be the best approach available.

This book contains many complex and lengthy diagrams, but as an editorial policy I have included as many positions from actual games as possible. I made every effort to cover all the major tesuji patterns and tactical patterns, but I acknowledge there are limits. There are many diagrams I reluctantly had to leave out.

Even so, I added 30 more diagrams than originally planned. Is it sentimentality, or affection? I feel there is not a single diagram I could bear to discard.

Three types of indexes are provided at the back of the book for reference. Furthermore, for related diagrams, cross-references are indicated from the perspectives of both similar shapes and identical tesuji.

Since I took on the work of editing this encyclopedia, the years have come and gone many times and the winds have shifted — yet my conviction that I wanted to compile a useful reference for both those who look things up and those who study has never wavered in the slightest. Looking back on the time I poured generously into each step without rushing, I feel things I cannot fully put into words.

I would be glad if you find this book useful for looking up individual diagrams.

April 2018

Morita Noboru

Editorial Notes
  1. This book is intended for intermediate through dan-level players, and has been compiled as an encyclopedia by carefully selecting 668 diagrams of tesuji patterns and tactical patterns that frequently arise in actual games.
  2. Classification and categorization are based on shape, or on the line number of the cut at the key point.
  3. Arrangement follows similarity as the first priority, and as the second priority proceeds from edge-of-board positions to central positions.
  4. Where necessary, variant diagrams, reference diagrams, and sequence diagrams are included.
  5. For related diagrams, cross-references are indicated from the perspectives of both similar shapes and identical tesuji.
  6. Important diagrams (fundamental patterns and frequently occurring shapes) are marked with ＊.
  7. Three types of indexes have been compiled and appended at the back of the book: a classification index, a common-shape index, and a subject index.
  8. Explanations are kept to a minimum, noting points to bear in mind in actual games and similar matters.
