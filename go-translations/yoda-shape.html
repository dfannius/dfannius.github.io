Preface

About a year ago, I opened the "Yoda Juku." Through direct contact with amateur players, I came to understand that there is much more they don’t understand than I had imagined, and I also came to understand what exactly they don’t understand.

So I researched how to teach in a way that would lead to understanding and improvement, and the game record sheets on which I jotted notes every time a teaching idea came to me came to number in the thousands. More recently, I have grasped the knack for getting students to understand, and I have grown quite confident in my teaching.

As the fruit of that research, I have put together this book.

Go books published up to now have been written on the assumption that the reader will know at least a certain amount. But I have found that players often do not understand even that much, so I have made it my aim to explain things in thorough and careful detail.

This book is for:

  • Those who have learned the rules of Go but don’t know what comes next.
  • Those who can’t seem to get stronger.
  • Those who want to build a solid foundation.

I hope that everyone who wants to improve will read this book. I can guarantee that mastering its content will transform your game. Please continue to enjoy playing Go.

Early Summer 2011

Yoda Norimoto

1. Efficiency and Possibility

Go is a game where "the player with more territory wins." However, if you simply keep surrounding territory, you will lose quickly. This is because moves that do nothing but surround territory make poor use of each individual stone.

There are two things that matter in Go: "stone efficiency" and "possibility." If you improve your stone efficiency and move toward the side with more possibilities, you will certainly win.

Efficient stones are described as "beautiful shapes." A beautiful shape is one in which there are no wasted stones. Conversely, inefficient stones are called "bad shapes."

The Significance of Knowing Tesuji

In my experience, many amateurs who call themselves 5-dan or 6-dan do not know tesuji. Or rather, I have almost never met an amateur who truly understands the principles of Go.

To improve your strength, you need to learn correct Go thinking and tesuji. The opposite of tesuji is crude play. Playing purely by instinct results in crude play. That is why it is necessary to know tesuji.

For example, a move that would be ideal if you could play two consecutive moves is often a bad move. "Wishful reading" may also be the work of instinct. "Wishful reading" — imagining your opponent’s moves in a way convenient for yourself — is not truly "reading." Reading means considering what you and your opponent both believe to be your best moves.

I hope you will use this book to learn correct thinking and correct tesuji, and to come to know beautiful form.

What Does “Move from Your Weak Stones” Mean?

Weak stones are important stones that are not yet alive — stones that cannot be sacrificed. Stones that are fine to be captured are called junk stones and do not fall into the category of weak stones.

The proverb "don’t approach thickness" originally meant don’t approach the opponent’s strong stones, but approaching your own strong stones is never beneficial either. Moving from strong stones results in a heavy shape — that is, an inefficient formation.

Remember: "don’t enclose thickness, don’t approach it."
