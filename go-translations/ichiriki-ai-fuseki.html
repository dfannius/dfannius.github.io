Large-Point Theory

The key to good fuseki play is identifying the large points of value. In this chapter, in addition to the flow of practical large-point selection, we will learn fundamental ideas such as playing toward open areas.

Theme Diagram 1
Open-Skirt Sense

The most immediately actionable way to identify high-value large points is to pay attention to the open skirt.

An open skirt is a place where room for reduction remains. There are two principles for applying this idea to fuseki: ① Lead the opponent’s stones to open-skirt areas. ② Avoid playing in open-skirt areas yourself.

For example, even if you expand your own position, if the means to reduce it remain, it becomes difficult to regard that area as a large piece of territory.

Sense of Stone Direction

The major branching factor in fuseki is joseki selection. What criterion should guide the choice? The key consideration, in large part, is the direction of stones.

To put it plainly: what matters is expanding your sphere of influence (or moyo) in the direction where greater territory can be expected.

Of course, knowledge of joseki and its application are required, and putting this into practice is not easy. However, with a proper sense of stone direction, you will rarely play a fuseki that misses the mark.

A Practical Perspective

Large-point selection is a difficult area with many ambiguous criteria and a heavy reliance on intuition.

The basic fuseki principle taught is play in open areas, but in practice the downside is that areas that are too open are easily invaded, so it cannot always be said that openness is justice. Rather, there are cases where securing firm territory in a narrower area is better.

While there are differences between basic principles and actual play, modern Go strongly tends to demand identifying land that can become settled territory easily (or playing in a way that denies the opponent settled territory). The sense is that the lens of whether an investment will yield high returns has become sharper than in the past.

Let’s work through the problems while encountering various theories, and develop a feel for selecting large points.

Why Differences in Strength Arise

In Go, the skills and knowledge required change gradually with each level. This is because as your strength increases, the range of what you can understand expands. As one example, let’s examine the basic fuseki principle of playing in open areas.

For players from kyu level up through dan, choosing an open area earns a passing mark. This is because heading toward places that tend to be correct matters more than subtle technique.

High-dan players and above, by contrast, factor in local reading and knowledge to ask: How open can I play? Wouldn’t it be better to play more narrowly?

The source of this difference in thinking is the possibility of playing narrowly. It runs counter to basic instinct, but there are many practical examples where playing narrowly is the superior approach. Understanding this way of thinking opens up even more options.

This is the difference that arises from the range of what one can understand. Aiming for a higher level may seem daunting, but by learning one thing at a time through this book’s content and your own practical experience, the quality of your fuseki will improve - so build up steadily without rushing.

Tips for AI Research

When researching openings with AI, the most important thing is to "look for moves you can actually use." This is because understanding the top candidate moves is often difficult in itself.

In summary, if you satisfy two conditions — "examining moves you can actually play" and "using 100,000 or more playouts" — you should be able to find moves that work in real games, whether you are a professional or amateur. How to Handle Mistakes

Playing perfectly throughout a game is extremely difficult. Even the strongest players make mistakes. That is precisely why it is important to have "a strategy that assumes mistakes and failures."

What we want you to practice is this: "Do not compound a single mistake with further failures." A small loss can be recovered, but accumulated losses can lead to a situation impossible to recover from.

A typical mistake is trying to turn things around in the vicinity of where you went wrong. This may work sometimes, but in most cases it widens the wound and worsens the position. First, try considering the option of leaving that area alone and heading elsewhere. This should offer a hint for preventing the position from deteriorating further.

Also, a game played to the end can exceed 300 moves. Even if you fall nearly 10 points behind in the middle game, closing the gap by one point per move in the endgame means you can make it up in about 20 moves. Stay calm, wait for your chance, and you will expand the possibility of a long-term reversal.
