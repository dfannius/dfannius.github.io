Preface

Knowing how to count points makes an enormous difference in how you play a game of Go. Beginners and intermediate players especially often proceed by estimation rather than calculation. If their estimates and instincts are correct, that is fine, but they are often mistaken.

This book is based on Introduction to Go Calculation published in 2006, with additional material on how to think about large endgame moves in actual play.

Chapter 1 explains the method of calculation. The method itself is straightforward. Use it to develop your way of thinking about the value of each move.

Chapter 2 studies various positions to ask: "How many points is the next move?" The basics are as learned in Chapter 1, but some calculations will be fairly complex. If a calculation seems too complicated, I recommend estimating the value roughly on your own.

Chapter 3 explains thinking about large endgame moves using examples from my own games. I hope you will gain an understanding of which moves are urgent.

Chapter 4 presents a reference table focused mainly on endgame moves. All calculations have been omitted, so please investigate anything you find doubtful on your own.

I hope this book will serve as a guide for playing a game of Go, and the endgame in particular.

9-dan Ishida Yoshio

First, let’s briefly learn how to calculate the value of each move.

Every move has a definite value. There are moves of great value and moves of small value. Whether large or small, the basic method of calculation is the same.

Most beginners and intermediate players seem to play without knowing any method of calculation. They play by feel, without knowing the correct approach.

Once you master the method of calculation, you will be able to play a different kind of Go - a "bright" game. Rather than groping blindly through the dark, let us walk the right path with calculation as our weapon.

Diagram 5: Whether Black or White plays this shape, it is sente - as shown in Diagrams 6 and 7. A position where either player’s move is sente is called double sente.

  • Double gote (gote for either player)
  • One-sided sente (sente for one side, gote for the other)
  • Double sente (sente for either player)

Every move belongs to one of these three categories.

Of the three, the order of priority is as follows:

  1. Double sente
  2. One-sided sente
  3. Double gote

Double sente is "first come, first served," so it is the highest priority. Next comes one-sided sente, and finally double gote.

However, this is a general rule - playing a double-sente move too early can sometimes eliminate aji, and there are also situations where even a double-gote move worth 20 or more points must be played immediately.

Calculating Difficult Moves

All the positions we have examined so far are endgame moves with clearly defined shapes. Calculating those is relatively easy. But moves in the middle game, or endgame positions where the shape is not yet settled - moves carrying various threats (attacking threats, etc.) or latent possibilities - are hard to evaluate in terms of exact point value.

This is especially true of opening moves, where accurate point counting is virtually impossible.

Even so, it is possible to arrive at a rough estimate of how many points a move is worth. The basic method is to calculate the difference depending on whether each side plays the move, and then determine whether it is sente or gote. The basics are simple.

Thinking About Large Endgame Moves

Up to this point, we have focused primarily on calculating the point value of endgame moves. Of course, it is normally important to play the largest moves first, but in large endgame situations, there are cases where other considerations take priority over point count. In this chapter, I explain how to think about large endgame moves, using examples from my own games. Before playing what seems like a large move, please try to keep the following four points in mind.

Four Points for Large Endgame Moves

  1. Moves with follow-up threats (moves that affect groups’ bases) are large.

  2. Moves that enclose the center are often deferred.

  3. Consider whether you can push one step further.

  4. Look for sequences that allow sente endgame moves.

Ishida Yoshio

Born in 1948 in Aichi Prefecture. Became a student of 9-dan Kitani Minoru at age 9 in 1957. Turned professional in 1963; reached 9-dan in 1974.

Won the 12th Prime Minister’s Cup in 1968. Won the inaugural Rising Stars Tournament in 1969. Won the 17th Japan Go Association Championship in 1970 and defended it in the 18th. In 1971, in the 26th Honinbo match, defeated Rin Kaiho to become Honinbo at age 22, the youngest ever at that time; subsequently defended the title five consecutive times. In 1974, in the 13th Meijin match, defeated Rin Kaiho to become the third player to hold both the Meijin and Honinbo titles simultaneously. Won the Tengen title in 1984. Two-time winner of the Ooteai Division 1 and the Pro Top Ten. Won the inaugural Japan Series in 1973. Won the Oza title in 1974 (22nd) and 1978 (26th). Three-time Hayago Championship winner. Won the NHK Cup in 1987 (34th), 1990 (37th), and 2001 (48th). Runner-up in the 2002 Hayago Championship. Won the 7th NEC Cup Tournament in 1988, and was the inaugural IBM Cup champion in 1988.

Three-time recipient of the Shuusai Prize and two-time recipient of the Kido Award for Best Player. In 1985 received the TV Go Program Producers’ Award. Known by the nickname "The Computer." From 2008, holds the honorary title of 24th Honinbo Shuho. In 2010, achieved 1,000 career wins.
