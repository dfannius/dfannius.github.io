Preface

Go is said to be "ever-changing, with no two games ever alike throughout history." Even in life-and-death situations, which are just one part of the game, the positions and shapes that arise are limitless, and it is very common to misjudge how to respond in actual games.

It goes without saying, but life-and-death situations in actual games — especially those involving joseki variations — are characterized by a surprising number of techniques, owing to the territorial appearance of the shapes involved. Moreover, even when shapes look similar, the vital point of the eye shape shifts with a slight difference, and the killing sequences can differ greatly. Unless you understand the proper technique very accurately, you will find yourself confused in actual games.

With this in mind, I have carefully selected over 600 life-and-death positions that frequently arise in actual games, arranged them in an orderly sequence, and organized them so that differences in shape and similarities and differences in technique can be compared and examined. Furthermore, to enhance its function as an encyclopedia, I have included two types of indices at the end of the volume for easy reference.

Since I have also put it in problem format, it can be solved one problem at a time as tsumego. It is not necessary to be able to solve every problem in this book — I hope readers will memorize the shapes. When a similar shape arises in an actual game, if you develop the sense to think "I wonder if there’s something here…" — that is what will lead to improvement in playing strength.

Finally, it would make me very happy if readers would keep this small encyclopedia close at hand and make frequent use of it.

December 1984

Kato Masao

Editor’s Preface

Compiling a Go encyclopedia is an extremely difficult undertaking. For example, if positions are arranged by coordinate notation, the relationships between them are lost. Classification by shape seems desirable, but one encounters situations where classification is impossible.

Circumstances are endless, from the major elements down to the minute details, and no one would believe it possible to compile a perfect, flawless encyclopedia.

Setting aside the ideal, I had no choice but to proceed through trial and error in compiling this book. "While it is obvious that you cannot make an index without first arranging the positions, you equally cannot arrange the positions without actually creating the index." Reaching a resolution required many twists and turns.

As encyclopedia functions, I classified and arranged positions by shape, and added cross-references for related diagrams. As needed, I included pattern diagrams, reference diagrams, and sequence diagrams, and additionally provided a classification index and a standard-shape index at the end of the volume.

Many years have passed since I became involved in compiling this encyclopedia, and though the winds of the seasons have shifted, my conviction to "compile an encyclopedia useful to both those seeking references and those conducting research" has not wavered in the slightest. Looking back on the time I devoted unhurriedly and without reservation to each stage, I am filled with feelings that cannot be fully expressed. I would be happy if readers make use of this book for looking up positions.

November 2021

Morita Noboru

Conventions

  1. This book is aimed at intermediate to advanced players, and has been compiled as an encyclopedia by carefully selecting approximately 600 life-and-death positions that commonly arise in actual games.

  2. Commentary is kept to the necessary minimum, showing only the forms considered viable as practical techniques.

  3. Classification and categorization are based primarily on the internal shape of the life-and-death position.

  4. Arrangement is organized first by similarity, second from smaller to larger life-and-death forms, and third from those with a narrower interior to those with a wider interior.

  5. The life-and-death positions included are of five types: Black to play, White dies; Black to play, ko results; Black to play, Black lives; living positions (Black to play, White lives); and dead positions (White to play, White dies). Capturing races are excluded. Note that living positions (including seki) and dead positions are indicated on the problem diagram.

  6. For ko positions, the distinction between direct ko, approach ko, and similar types is indicated.

  7. As needed, pattern diagrams, reference diagrams, and sequence diagrams are included, and cross-references ("See also") are indicated to facilitate research and lookup.

  8. Important life-and-death diagrams (fundamental types and frequently occurring forms) are marked with ＊.

  9. Two types of indices have been prepared — a classification index and a standard shape index — allowing lookup from either.
