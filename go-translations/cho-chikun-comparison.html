Preface

One characteristic of Go is that before a stone is placed on the board, all stones have equal value. It is only once a stone is played that each stone takes on a role, and some become more valuable while others contribute little.

If you go out of your way to save a stone lying idle by the roadside while abandoning one as precious as a diamond, you cannot win the game. Always be aware of which stones matter. The value of a move changes dramatically depending on the surrounding circumstances. In this book, we have collected problems where the value of a move changes based on subtle differences in the arrangement of stones. Compare A and B and think about which is bigger. You will find that with only the slightest difference, the value of a move can change completely.

In this book, we use monetary amounts as a rough guide to a move’s value. The correct answer is generally worth "10,000 yen," while wrong answers are often "1,000 yen" or "100 yen." Even if a move falls short of the correct answer, a reasonably large move may be worth "5,000 yen" or "3,000 yen." Rather than simply thinking about whether a move is right or wrong, considering how much value it has will make the problems even more enjoyable.

As with other books in the "At a Glance" series, please solve the problems in this book repeatedly.

November 2013

Cho Chikun, 25th Honinbo
