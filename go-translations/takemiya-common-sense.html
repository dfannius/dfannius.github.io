Foreword

I have never liked the word "common sense." According to the Kōjien dictionary, "common sense" refers to knowledge that ordinary people have or ought to have. But to me, it has an unpleasant image — as if it binds people and forces them into a mold. What passes as "common sense" in Japan is not necessarily the same in Mongolia. Human beings are fundamentally free; as long as you don’t cause trouble for others, you should be able to do whatever you want. It’s your own life, not anyone else’s. Rather than being constrained by the mold of common sense, it’s better to take personal responsibility for everything and judge for yourself what is best and what course of action to take.

That said, in this book I have deliberately used the word "common sense" to explain what I would at minimum like amateurs to learn. My hope is that you will first come to understand what "common sense" means in the context of this book.

Life is meant to be enjoyed. Go is the same as life. Playing the moves you want to play is the most fun, and that is the secret to improvement that applies to everything.

I believe all of you amateurs are playing because you want to get stronger, but you seem to play only safe moves out of fear of losing. That is tantamount to playing in a way that prevents you from getting stronger. When your spirit shrinks, your mind shrinks too. What matters is honestly and courageously playing the moves you want to play. If you do, you will feel as though you have suddenly grown stronger — as though the whole board has suddenly opened up before you. Play with a relaxed mindset, without fixating on winning or losing, and play the moves you want to play — and no matter what game you find yourself in, it will become enjoyable.

And after a few years, the gap in strength between those who play the moves they want to play and those who remain timid forever…

4

the gap in strength will become strikingly evident.

Life is the same way — if you remain timid, nothing will be any fun. In golf as in Go, when victory comes into view, you tend to tighten up. In golf, when you need to sink a one-putt to win, even a 50-centimeter putt suddenly becomes impossible. Whether it’s Go, dancing, or music, the mental experience is all the same. The best thing is to let yourself glide smoothly and naturally into that world.

I’m currently absorbed in dancing. I’ve been at it for two years. I don’t mind at all that I can’t dance well yet — that’s only natural. There’s no such thing as someone who is skilled from the start. But when even the smallest thing goes right, I find myself genuinely thinking "ah, I can do it now." I believe that feeling of "ah, this is fun" is tremendously important. Nothing in life gets started unless you enjoy it.

I can say this with confidence, because I’ve learned it through Go: your attitude matters more than your intelligence.

In this book, I use games from amateur players as material to explain why certain moves are good and why others are bad. Go is about applying what you know. I believe that if you read this book, come away with a real understanding of it, and apply it to your own games, you will surely see your strength improve.

1　The Shortcut to Getting Stronger at Go
◆ Is Losing Good for You?

If you say "it’s better to lose at Go," all one hundred out of a hundred people will surely say "What? That’s awful!"

But losing is by no means a bad thing. To get stronger, losing might actually be the better outcome. When you lose, you will review your game so as not to repeat the same mistakes, and that is far better than winning by luck and not reflecting on it.

For the same reason, playing a bad move is not bad for your improvement. What is bad is playing a poor shape and mistaking it for a good one. The real problem is not being able to recognize bad shape as bad and good shape as good.

◆ Study Methods for Getting Stronger

The question of "how can I get stronger at Go?" is an eternal theme that everyone shares.

The best method of improvement is to play the moves you want to play and then review that game (i.e., do a post-game analysis). The goal is to avoid repeating the same mistakes. Even just the first 50 moves of a game you played badly will do. If possible, having a stronger player look over your game is even more effective.

Professionals too, after each game, go over what went well and what went poorly.

Other methods for studying on your own include: ① solving simple tsumego, ② learning basic joseki (it’s fine if you forget them), and ③ replaying game records.

① Solve Simple Tsumego

Tsumego is training to help you instantly recognize shape and the correct line of play. There is no way around memorizing them. Look at a tsumego and if after two or three minutes you can’t figure it out…

9

look up the answer. Thinking any longer than that is a waste of time. Spending time on reading doesn’t accomplish much.

After going through problems several times, your body naturally memorizes the shapes, and the correct lines and vital points begin to become visible. The next time a similar problem comes up, you will find you can apply what you learned and solve it.

That said, for those who feel unsatisfied unless they work through a problem to the very end on their own, this method is not for them. It’s a matter of temperament, so there’s nothing to be done — but perhaps being too serious about everything isn’t always a good thing.

② Learn Basic Joseki (It’s Fine to Forget Them)

Studying joseki is training to help you develop a feel for good and bad shape, so the basics are enough. There is no need to memorize difficult joseki like the large diagonal joseki or the large avalanche joseki.

I myself have no desire to play difficult joseki in actual games. I absolutely don’t want to imitate others, and I have no interest in the endless fixed exchanges that joseki demand, or in being forced into moves I don’t want to play. Above all, it wouldn’t be fun. Playing Go that only you can play — that is what makes it enjoyable.

③ Replaying Game Records

Replaying game records is training to learn what it means to have a beautiful sense of the game. What matters is cultivating the good sensibility to recognize beautiful things as beautiful.

◆ The Reason Why, to Get Stronger, “You Must Play the Moves You Want to Play”

Many of you seem to be so preoccupied with wanting to win, or with not playing bad moves, that you end up unable to play the way you want.

But to improve, playing a bad move or making a mistake is not a bad thing.

There is no one who plays only good moves from the start. Professionals, of course, don’t play only good moves either. They study hard precisely to avoid playing bad moves.

10

Unless you play the moves you want to play — moves that feel right to you — you will never get much stronger no matter how many games you play. If you clear away the petty thoughts like "I don’t want to lose" or "I don’t want to be made a fool of," you should be able to play the moves you want.

◆ People Who Can Get Stronger, and People Who Can’t

Children are innocent. They don’t have the kind of pride that adults cling to — worrying that a move might make them look foolish. That may be why children have a higher chance of getting stronger.

But it’s not only children — among adults who have just started playing Go, and of course among those currently studying, there are people who seem likely to get stronger in the future. They are the type described below.

  • Innocent, cheerful people with an honest and pure heart
  • Positive thinkers
  • People who don’t give up even after losing
  • People who don’t see difficulty as a burden
  • People who don’t dread post-game reviews but can switch their mindset and approach them as something enjoyable

People of this type can get stronger in the future, even if it’s just self-delusion — by convincing themselves "I’m going to get stronger, I am getting stronger."

On the other hand, no matter how intelligent they may be, people who convince themselves "I have no talent, so I can’t do it" — those with a warped, negative outlook — may reach a certain level, but will not improve beyond that.

I myself — though I say so with some embarrassment — am very quick at switching mindsets. In that regard, I might even be a genius!? I forget things instantly. It’s probably because every day is so enjoyable. I’m so busy with enjoyable things that I have no time to dwell on unpleasant ones.

◆ What Matters More Than the “Winning and Losing” Right in Front of You

All of you amateurs, when it comes to "losing," have a strong…

11

negative association with losing. To put it dramatically, it’s as if they have a strange feeling that they have committed some kind of crime in real life. So when they win, they’re elated, and when they lose, they look despondent. Some of them, after losing even a single game, won’t play again for quite some time.

Of course, anyone is happy to win. But pros may look unsatisfied even after winning, and remain perfectly composed after a loss.

In my case in particular, my fundamental feeling is that what matters is playing each game carefully and improving myself - so I don’t care at all about winning or losing in the immediate sense. I’ve always been detached when it comes to competition, and the reason I still keep a calm face after losing is that I perhaps have a little too much of that quality.

◆ The “Wall” You Must Overcome

I have said that post-game review is essential for getting stronger, but for many of you, this may be a painful exercise. However, unless you overcome that hurdle, you cannot get stronger.

There is always a reason for a loss. It is important to understand which of your moves were bad, and why they were bad. For example, moving a stone that is already completely alive, or being too fixated on territory - these are often the kinds of reasons involved. But it is very hard to see these things on your own. That is why having a stronger player review the game with you is so effective.

When someone points out your mistakes and you think "ah, I see!" - all you need to do is correct those mistakes going forward. It is important to approach this with an open and honest mindset.

2. “Tendencies and Countermeasures” for Amateurs Who Want to Get Stronger
① The Amateur Tendency
◆ Amateurs Are Bundles of Greed

Amateurs have a powerful compulsion: take what can be taken, try to take what cannot be taken, and hate having their own stones captured. Even though creating heavy or bad shapes is a far greater sin, they will readily create such shapes and still strive with all their might to get what they want.

Wanting everything is greed. In life, if you are in good health, you can do anything. The most important thing is that your body is healthy.

The same is true in Go. If you are physically weak, you will not be able to live a happy life later on. Not being able to live a happy life translates, in Go terms, to losing.

As long as you are healthy, you can always make a comeback. In Go, being "healthy" means playing solidly and maintaining good shape. This usually means being in a position with relatively little territory.

Someone who is "frequently ill" in Go terms could also be described as enormously wealthy. When you have a lot of territory from the start, thin and weak spots will appear throughout your position.

But that too is a question of balance, and it is what makes Go interesting - just like life. Some people are perfectly comfortable playing in a "frequently ill" style, and everyone has their own way of thinking. Ultimately, it comes down to a matter of taste.

I am the type who thinks it is enough to be healthy. I have no desire to eat particularly fine food or wear expensive clothes. I have no desires at all. There is just one thing: I want to live enjoyably, without causing trouble to others.

◆ Amateurs Are Too Fixated on Their Own Plans

Go is constantly changing, so you must adapt flexibly and respond to each situation as it arises. For amateurs, however, this turns out to be surprisingly difficult.

They draw up a blueprint in their head, and once they have decided on a course of action, they cannot stop midway - they charge ahead regardless of the consequences. They attack when the position does not call for it, and as a result end up getting their own stones captured.

◆ Amateurs Reach Toward Where the Stones Clatter

Amateurs have an irresistible tendency to play where stones have just landed. Their eyes are drawn to small local areas, and they become overly focused there.

◆ The Four Instincts Amateurs Have

As I have been saying, most of you are greedy, overly fixed in your thinking, and tend to play wherever the last stone landed. Now let us consider this from a different angle - that of instinct.

Looking at actual games by high-dan amateurs, I identified four instincts you have in common. They are as follows.

a. An urge to surround territory

Because the desire for territory is so strong, even when a group is already alive and strong, they play moves to wring even more territory out of it.

b. Not knowing their place

Because they cannot assess the overall situation, they misjudge their position: fighting aggressively when their own stones are weak, or conversely, playing timidly when they are strong.

c. Worrying more than necessary

Because they misjudge the value of individual moves, they waste moves in places that don’t need attention - places an opponent would never bother playing.

d. Insensitivity to shape

Not knowing that making many efficient moves and good shapes is how you win, they make foolish

14

moves, perfectly content to let bad shapes develop.

② Countermeasures for Getting Stronger
◆ Go Has Certain Patterns!?

Go is often thought to be difficult, but in a sense it is a simple game. You are free to play however you like, but once you have played a stone, situations arise where you must respond in a specific way. For example: honoring the intent of a previously played stone, or not moving away from a stone that is already alive.

In other words, all you need to master is the idea of "if this, then that."

◆ But First…

Correct situational judgment is crucial in Go. You need to grasp the situation accurately and think about what the right move is.

When you misjudge the situation, your moves will be completely wrong. The reason is that there are many different approaches depending on the position - whether to fight forcefully, to make sabaki, to play lightly, or to sacrifice a stone.

◆ Developing a “Beautiful Way of Thinking”

Another important thing in playing Go is to have good thinking - what I call a "beautiful way of thinking."

For example: seeing a good shape and feeling it is beautiful; seeing a bad shape and feeling it is unpleasant; recognizing a large point as large. That is what "beautiful thinking" means.

If you feel a small point is large, something is wrong, and if a bad shape looks good to you, that is a problem. In that case, you need to correct your instincts and judgment.

3. Four Pieces of Common Sense Needed to Get Stronger
1. Play Where Your Opponent Wants to Play

Go is a game of "playing the most important point first." Since you alternate moves one at a time, you must always aim for the most valuable point available.

But amateurs tend to play moves that aren’t particularly needed — moves that are effectively passing a turn. What’s more, they don’t even realize they are passing, and casually play unnecessary moves inside their own territory. The cause is probably that they have difficulty distinguishing what is important from what is not.

2. Territory Is Not Enclosed

Go is a game where "the player with more territory at the end wins." To put it more precisely, it is a game of "thinking about how to end up with more territory."

Many players are attached to territory - wanting territory, feeling they must make territory. But territory is the final result; whether you make territory along the way or not is something you don’t need to worry too much about.

What matters in Go is whether you have many good shapes and whether your stones are placed at important points. Having stones in unnecessary places makes it easy to lose. You could even say there is no need to count territory in the middle of the game. All you need to do is make good shapes and avoid making bad ones. The best move…

20

Just play the best move - there is no other way. If playing your best still leads to a bad result, it just means the earlier moves were bad.

A common flaw among amateurs is playing many moves that merely extend the territory of already-living stones. It is like a healthy person taking supplements to get even stronger. When there are weak people who need attention, it does no good for the healthy person to get even healthier.

In short, you should focus on your weak stones (= your stones that have not yet secured life), while there is no need to defend your strong stones (= your stones that are already alive). Playing where you are already strong is inefficient.

The phrase "being tight on territory" (地にカライ) that you often hear does not simply mean valuing territory for its own sake. The premise is that by playing in positions related to both sides’ bases, you make your attacks on the opponent effective.

3. Know Your Station

Go is a game where "every game produces a different position." No one - professional or amateur - has ever played the same game twice. The shape of the board is determined entirely by the move-by-move exchange between you and your opponent. The best move in any given situation is the one with the best balance. "Balance" encompasses many things: the local effectiveness of stones, the overall balance of power, and more.

To be more specific: survey the whole board, and if your side has the stronger position, you can take a more assertive stance; if you have many weak or thin stones, you must play more carefully. If, despite having many weak stones, you come out aggressively, your weak stones will naturally end up under attack.

It helps to think of it in terms of your own physical health. You always know whether you are feeling well or unwell - you are aware of your own condition. But amateurs have a completely chaotic sense of this: many have no idea whether they are well or sick. As a result, they push too hard when they are already in poor shape, and things fall apart. If you tend to be sickly, you need to behave accordingly.

The same is true in Go.

4. Do Not Make Bad Shapes

Go is fundamentally a game of "if bad shapes appear, your position deteriorates, and if you make many good shapes, you win" - yet surprisingly few people know this. The rules have not changed. Thinking that it is fine to have bad shapes as long as you have more territory is a mistake. This is because bad shapes are inefficient plays.

Rather than keeping stones alive in an awkward or heavy shape, it is sometimes better to sacrifice them instead. If they are captured in a terrible way, the game is lost, but there are also methods of deliberately sacrificing them to create good overall shape.

28

The key in Go is to play efficient moves, and if you play efficient moves, good shapes result. In practice, it may be hard to believe that "having bad shapes alone means losing." But if many bad shapes accumulate, you will lose in the end.

In Go, it is important not to let individual shapes collapse - remember that if your shapes break down, you will lose. I would really like you to become much more sensitive to shape.

At commentary sessions for world championships, I often say "this is already over" after looking at just the first 20 or so moves of the opening, and I am almost always right. That is because I am judging by the shapes.

Even if you make many bad shapes, if your opponent plays terribly afterward you may still win, but if the game is played correctly you should lose. In games between amateurs, a bad shape may not immediately decide things, but among professionals you will almost certainly not win.

Professionals dislike having their shapes broken above all else. They have a strict eye when it comes to good and bad shape.

By contrast, amateurs do not pay much attention to shape. All they want is territory and to capture stones, with little thought given to making good shape. There are truly many people who do not know that bad shapes make you more likely to lose. If you make good shapes, you will naturally win…

Good shapes and bad shapes are things you simply have to memorize. For example, the "double peep at a bamboo joint" is the classic bad shape - because you are peeping at a point that cannot be cut. It is also important to understand why something is bad and why something is good.

4 Summary: Conditions for Becoming Strong
◆ The type of person who can become strong

Someone who is positive about everything, and can shift their mindset even through hardship and keep persevering.

◆ Study methods for becoming strong

① Play the moves you want without fear of losing → Reflect after the game → Don’t repeat the same mistakes

② Solve easy life-and-death problems

③ Study basic joseki (forgetting is fine)

④ Replay game records

◆ A beautiful way of thinking about Go

Survey the whole board, assess the situation, and avoid making bad shapes.

◆ To become strong, discard the “amateur mindset” and acquire “proper common sense.”

① Amateur mindset: Overly anxious. Proper common sense: No need to play where your opponent won’t play.

② Amateur mindset: Wants to make territory. Proper common sense: Don’t be greedy for territory.

③ Amateur mindset: Overestimates themselves. Proper common sense: Know your place.

④ Amateur mindset: Insensitive to shape. Proper common sense: Don’t make bad shapes.

Takemiya Masaki

Born January 1, 1951, in Tokyo. Studied under Tanaka Sanshichi 7-dan, then entered the school of Kitani Minoru 9-dan in 1965, turning professional the same year. Reached 9-dan in 1977. One of the "Three Birds of the Kitani School" alongside Ishida Yoshio 9-dan and Kato Masao 9-dan. At age 17 in 1968, placed eighth in the 5th Pro Juketsu Tournament. First entered the Honinbo League in 1971. Won the 31st Honinbo title in 1976 and held the Honinbo title for a cumulative six terms through 1988. Won the Fujitsu Cup World Championship in consecutive years 1988 and 1989. Won the NHK Cup in 1989; won the Judan title for the first time in 1990 and defended in 1991 and 1992. Won the Kakusei title for the first time in 1991. Won the Meijin title in 1995. Four-time consecutive winner of the TV Asia Championship. Two-time winner of the Prime Minister’s Cup, one-time winner of the Speed Go Championship, winner of First Division of the Grand Tournament twice and Second Division once, two-time winner of the NEC Cup. Received the Shuya Award once, the Journalist Club Award, the Television Go Program Production Award, and the Kido Award twelve times. His "cosmic style" continues to inspire amateur players. His son Yokou is a 5-dan professional.
