Preface

One commonly hears the lament, “That person plays an unrefined game, but is so strong you can’t handle them.” What does it mean to be strong? It means being superior to ordinary players at reading the tactical sequences of life-and-death situations and capturing races when engaged in battle. In other words, the elements of strength are one’s intuition for recognizing good sequences and the accuracy and depth of one’s reading.

Among those who play an unrefined game, many possess these special abilities without knowing anything about joseki.

Although many Go books explaining the fundamentals of joseki, fuseki, life and death, and endgame have been published, books for practicing these fighting techniques have been neglected, because it is difficult to capture and present the ever-changing forms and dynamics of battle.

Amateurs often ask, “Sensei, how many moves ahead can you read?” Sometimes one can read dozens of moves ahead, and sometimes one cannot see even a single move ahead, so it is impossible to give a simple answer. When the sequence follows a logical path, one can sometimes read the result dozens of moves ahead. But when standing at a crossroads of fuseki or strategy, there are cases where even a single move ahead is unclear.

Even after learning the standard fundamentals, without cultivating reading ability, one can hardly make full use of that acquired knowledge. Indeed, it is fair to say that most amateurs are crushed in one fell swoop when a critical juncture in battle arrives.

When asking professional teachers about methods for strengthening this ability, rather than training through actual games against stronger opponents…

3

…the most common answer is that there is no other way.

I had long wanted to try to achieve this kind of strength training through a book rather than through actual games. So I wrote this book. I have edited it with the intention that it serve as a substitute for a stronger opponent. I have taken pains that no one would suspect to arrange the positions not as isolated local situations but across the full board, just as in real games, so that they can be viewed as beautiful, well-formed shapes. The explanations are also written to be easy to read and understand, so I believe that while studying this book, one will find the research enjoyable and pleasant, and before long one’s reading will become more accurate and deeper, and one’s Go will grow stronger.

If you study two problems a day, I am confident that by the time you finish the first volume, advanced players will have improved by at least one stone, and intermediate and lower-level players will have improved by several stones as well.

The problems are classified into A, B, and C levels: A is intended as study material for high-dan players, B for dan-level players, and C for players around shodan. However, this is only a rough classification, and there is no need to be bound by it.
