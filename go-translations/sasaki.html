The Game of Go

The Shortcut to Getting Stronger

In this book, all chapter titles follow the format “Don’t do ○○,” and there is a clear intention behind this. It is because I believe that the shortcut to improvement for amateur players is not learning “what to do,” but learning “what not to do.” Many people may find this surprising, since most players tend to rush toward learning the correct moves — what the right play is in a given position.

So why do I consider learning “what not to do” to be the shortcut? I would like to share the reasoning upfront, as I think it will help the content that follows make more sense. Incidentally, this principle of “what not to do” applies regardless of whether you are in the opening, middle game, or endgame. Unlike detailed knowledge such as joseki or yose tesuji, it can be applied universally from the very first move through to the yose. This alone should give you a sense of how efficient an approach it is — it is, in fact, the very essence of Go, the “truly important things.” The prologue contains no board diagrams and is text only, but it is just two pages, so I hope you will bear with me.

9
The Nature of Go as a Game

To get stronger at Go, it is essential to understand the characteristics of Go as a game. I think of Go as a beautifully balanced game. Black and White alternate moves on equal footing, and the komi system adjusts for the advantage of playing first, making the game highly equitable. Given this, however, a natural question arises: “Why does the balance tip? Why does someone win and someone lose?” The answer is simple: “Because one side always makes a mistake.” Go has no such thing as a single brilliant move that turns the game around in an instant. Anyone who has played a bit of Go will likely understand this intuitively. It would not be an overstatement to say that there is no such thing as a “good move” in Go. Go is a game where both players alternate making moves that are either neutral — neither gaining nor losing — or negative, and the side with the larger total of negatives loses. It is, in other words, a penalty-based game. So what should you do to win at this penalty-based game, and what is the shortcut to improvement? The answer is: “eliminate bad moves” and “stop doing what you should not do.”

10

That said, many people may be unsure of what to do to reduce bad moves in their Go. This is where this book comes in. As mentioned, all chapter titles follow the format “Don’t do ○○.” The book organizes the most representative bad-move patterns in Go into seven categories, with the message that avoiding these alone will go a long way toward reducing the negative moves in your game. The types of moves that are absolutely fatal in Go are, perhaps surprisingly, quite few. If you faithfully follow the seven “Don’t do ○○” principles introduced in this book, clear-cut bad moves will almost disappear from your game at the amateur level. Conversely, this also means that obvious bad moves arising from violations of these principles appear very frequently in amateur Go. So if you can understand the “what not to do” principles presented in this book and eliminate the bad moves that stem from them, you can create a situation where you are no longer playing bad moves, while your opponents continue to play them as before.

Please keep these two points firmly in mind — “Go is a penalty-based game” and “avoiding bad moves is the shortcut to improvement” — as you work through the “Don’t do ○○” chapters beginning on the next page. The theme diagrams are drawn from actual amateur games, so I think you will find the material approachable.

Fix Your Opening

One approach to quickly improving your win rate at Go is to fix how you play the opening. Surprisingly, many players decide on the fly whether to play star points or 3-4 points, which direction to orient a 3-4 point stone, and whether to play parallel or diagonal formations. With this approach, every game presents an unfamiliar opening position. And finding the right move in an unfamiliar opening position - let alone judging who stands better - is extremely difficult. Running into an unfamiliar shape early in the game, becoming flustered, losing your footing, and ending up in a complete defeat: this is an experience that many players have had.

The way to reduce these losses is to fix how you play the opening. Stars stay stars, 3-4 points stay 3-4 points. Then decide whether to approach your opponent’s star point or invade at 3-3; when approaching your opponent’s 3-4 point, decide whether to use a one-space jump, a small knight’s move, or a large knight’s move. By deciding these things in advance, you can dramatically reduce the chance of encountering unfamiliar positions in the opening. If you accumulate practical experience playing only the shapes you have decided on, the knowledge needed for your chosen openings will naturally and efficiently build up. If you have never fixed your opening before, please try starting today.

“Playing Atari-Atari Is the Hallmark of Weak Go”

“Playing atari-atari is the hallmark of weak go” - this is a proverb that everyone has surely heard at least once. Even when reviewing game records, I often see players who have developed a habit of reflexively playing atari and are losing a great deal because of it. I believe this happens because they cannot visualize, in terms of actual board positions, why playing atari is so bad and what benefits there are to not playing it. There are only three types of atari that are good to play in actual games:

  • Ataris that capture the opponent’s stones
  • Ataris that cut the opponent’s stones
  • Ataris that destroy the opponent’s shape

● Eyes Win Overwhelmingly Over Territory

Go is a game of competing for territory, but the important point is that “the player with more territory at the end wins.” It is not the case, for example, that having five more points of visible territory at move 30 earns bonus points. The way to end up with more territory is not to pursue territory too eagerly along the way.

To maximize territory at the end, it is important to make moves with “eyes” as your reference point. Even if you increase your territory by neglecting eyeless stones, the liability of those eyeless stones will eventually take effect, and your territory will shrink while your opponent’s grows. Playing only near stones that already have eyes leads to inefficient play - your territory will not increase and your opponent’s will not decrease. Rather than territory, let us develop the ability to think about moves with eyes as our reference point.

I never dreamed that the day would come when Iiyama-sensei would write a recommendation comment for my own book. I wish to take this opportunity to express my sincere gratitude to Iiyama-sensei for accepting my request, to Morishita-san and everyone at Mynavi Publishing for giving me the chance to write this book, and to my parents who supported my Go studies for 15 years even knowing I would never turn professional.

Column: Why I Do This Work

The reason I put so much effort into activities like YouTube and writing books is that I want to put to use what I have studied so hard to learn. During the period when I was competing in amateur tournaments, winning was my top priority and I thought of nothing else. But at some point, I began to find that deeply hollow. Win or lose, the only person affected was myself. Is this really okay? I began to wonder. Of course, I have the deepest respect for professional players and amateurs who pursue their own victories and beautiful Go and keep pushing themselves. But that was simply not what I wanted to do.

I suspect there are many people like me who want to share their love of Go with others or give back what they have learned. To those people, I say: please start with something, even if small. I too kept up my outreach work steadily, and finally managed to reach the long-awaited goal of publishing a book. I hope to have shown that even an ordinary amateur can achieve something like this if they keep at it. I would be delighted to see even more activity in support of the Go community going forward.
