The Evolution of Black and White

When Go was created in ancient China, it was different from today: it was the nobility and the stronger players who held the Black stones. The game records handed down from that era clearly show that White moved first.

The oldest Go textbook in Japan is the “Igo Shiki” (A Go Treatise), written by Genson, a Buddhist monk of the Kamakura period. Following Chinese custom, it states: “Sit before the board, open the stone container, give the Black stones to your opponent, and take the White stones yourself.” Giving the Black stones to the opponent was a form of showing respect.

It was not until the Muromachi period that this was reversed. Because lighting in those days was nothing like it is today, and games were usually played at night, the Black stones were hard to see clearly. In order to show respect to the nobility during such games, it became the custom to give the easier-to-see White stones to the opponent. However, in that period there was still no rule specifying whether Black or White would move first.

Entering the Edo period, judging from game records in the “Kiden-ki,” the first move of the leading player was played with White. Counting from the game between Sansa and Rigen during the Tensho era, there were twenty-six such games in total. Remarkably,

According to shogunate records, during the Keian era the new custom of “honoring White and belittling Black” was formally adopted, and the relative status of Black and White was officially reversed. The practice of Black moving first and White moving second may well have been brought about through official intervention.

Recently, the sumo world — which boasts a long history as a national sport — made a major decision to remove the four pillars from the sumo ring. Many wrestlers and conservatives were opposed, but after the pillars were removed the arena became more spacious and better suited for viewing. This was a wise and decisive act by the association’s leadership, and also a sign of the times.

In the early Meiji period, Master Murase Shuho revived and greatly promoted Go, ushering in the golden age of the Taisho and Showa eras. The postwar Nihon Ki-in is an institution with even greater aspirations to bring Go onto the world stage. In order to establish a footing throughout the world in the future, certain reforms must be undertaken and systems built. This is the true intention of those who love the game. An Olympic Competition of the Mind

Human development divides into physical growth and intellectual cultivation. Only when both aspects are fully developed can one be considered a truly complete person. Mere physical development without matching intellectual capacity, by current standards, is the mark of a person who is essentially useless.

The Olympic Games are entirely physical activities. That they have so far failed to include intellectual competitions is a great pity. I believe that physical education and intellectual education advancing together is the proper ideal. The competitions that fall under intellectual education include go, shogi, chess, gomoku, Chinese chess (xiangqi), and others - all very useful for training the brain.

In this spirit, I hope the Olympic Games can add competitions in the intellectual realm. Japan’s judo has already nearly become an Olympic event; I hope that in the future not only Japan’s go, but also chess, shogi, xiangqi, gomoku, and others can enter the competition. I believe this is not merely my personal view - people throughout the world must surely share this ideal!

This is a famous saying left by the 11th Inoue Gennan Inseki, admonishing players about the mental state one should maintain during a game - something well worth learning from.

When playing Go, there is always an opponent. If the opponent speaks rudely, one becomes intimidated and shrinks back. If the opponent is weak, one is tempted to bully him with unreasonable moves. When we study the game records of great players, we sometimes marvel that such brilliant players can exist in this world.

To achieve the state of “no stones on the board” - a thorough mastery of Go principles - is no easy matter. I believe one must work out the best moves before placing a stone; if you have to figure things out on the board, you cannot claim to have reached this level. Even those with the title of Meijin who can reach this level must still devote their whole life’s energy to cultivating the mental state of “no opponent before the board.” If one can achieve this, not only will one’s Go improve, but one’s interpersonal relationships will also harmonize!
