Good and Bad Middlegame Moves as Taught by Go AI
Anzai Nobuaki
2
Foreword

The advent of AI has dramatically changed how Go is played. In games between professionals, AI techniques are used almost without exception. New joseki variations such as early san-san invasions are particularly conspicuous, but that is not all — AI’s influence has also reached the middlegame. Unlike the opening, the middlegame never takes exactly the same shape, yet the way of thinking about it has been changing.

AI only shows lines of play; it does not explain its thinking. Nevertheless, we can read certain things from its tendencies. In this book, I have extracted positions where AI’s moves differ from conventional intuition, and from those I have summarized what I’ve interpreted as AI’s characteristics. Since the book is in problem format, please read it while trying to predict what AI would do.

AI is not perfect, but this is clearly an era in which it is stronger than humans. I believe that encountering the new world AI reveals and broadening one’s range of thinking is the surest path to improvement. It would make me very happy if this book can play some part in that.

In writing this book, I primarily used Leelazero #254.

62

Erasure and reduction are areas where AI excels. I think this is where the difference from humans shows most clearly.

As a characteristic, AI seems to prefer direct moves like the attach, shoulder hit, peep, and cut as its first candidates. When you are in a position of clear weakness, you have no choice but to take the attack. The idea is to create a shape that puts some burden on the opponent, and while they respond to it, build a formation that is hard to attack.

When direct approaches do not work, AI creates contrast by spacing two or more points away. A cap placed one point away often seems halfhearted. It is also a way of responding to the opponent. As a competitive strategy, giving the opponent a wide range of viable options means there are often good moves among them. That said, between humans, offering a broad selection - moves that create difficult positions, attacking moves, tenuki - presents the opponent with many hard decisions.
